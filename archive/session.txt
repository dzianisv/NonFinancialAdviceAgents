Good question — you've spotted the key issue. A "-7% from 52-week high" trigger isn't a single moment; it's the start of a process that could last days, weeks, or months. Here's how to think about execution.
The core problem with single-shot triggers
If you set "as soon as -7%, deploy $40K," three things can go wrong:

Whipsaw — Market touches -7% intraday Monday, bounces, you bought the wick, and now there's no dip to buy.
Premature exhaustion — You hit -7%, deploy Tier 1, market keeps falling to -12%, you deploy Tier 2, then -20%, you deploy Tier 3 — all within 3 weeks. You used your full reserve in a correction that turns into a full bear market dropping another 15%. No ammo left.
Anchoring to the wrong high — 52-week high resets as the market grinds up. A -7% trigger in a strong uptrend fires constantly on noise.

Better execution: layered limit orders within each tier
Instead of "at -7%, buy $40K in one click," split each tier into 3–4 sub-tranches with limit orders or weekly scheduled buys.

Rules that protect you from yourself
Don't skip tiers. If the market gaps down -15% overnight, deploy Tier 1 first (all of it, $40K), then Tier 2 on schedule. Resist the urge to "back up the truck" on day one.
Use weekly closes, not intraday lows. Most of your triggers should fire on Friday close prices. Intraday wicks (like flash crashes) reverse too often to be reliable signals.
Cooldown between tranches. Minimum 3 trading days between sub-tranche fills, even if conditions are met. Forces you to actually watch the dip develop.
Re-arm rule. If the market recovers above -5% from the new 52-week high, "reset" your tier counters. The next dip is a fresh dip. But don't let this trigger more than 2x per year — otherwise you're chasing noise.
Stop deploying if fundamentals crack. If you hit Tier 3 and we're in a recession with rising unemployment, VIX above 40, credit spreads blowing out — that's not necessarily a "buy more" signal, that's potentially a 2008-style scenario. Consider pausing the last sub-tranche and reassessing. This is judgment, not a rule.

Why this matters for tax-loss harvesting (TLH)
This is the actual reason I mentioned them in the earlier message. In a taxable account, here's the scenario:
You buy VOO at $690. Market drops to $580 (-16%). You're sitting on a $110/share unrealized loss. You want to:

Lock in the loss to offset gains elsewhere (or up to $3K of ordinary income per year)
Stay invested in the S&P 500 so you don't miss the recovery

The wash sale rule (IRS Section 1091) blocks you from claiming the loss if you buy a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. So you can't sell VOO and rebuy VOO.
The common workaround: sell VOO, immediately buy IVV or SPLG/SPYM. They track the same index but are issued by different companies, hold (technically) different share lots, and most tax advisors treat them as not substantially identical for wash-sale purposes — though the IRS has never explicitly ruled on ETF-to-ETF swaps of the same index.
Important caveat: The "not substantially identical" treatment for same-index ETFs from different issuers is the consensus interpretation in the tax community, but it's not officially blessed by the IRS. Some conservative tax advisors recommend swapping to a different index entirely (e.g., VOO → VTI, which tracks the total US market and has ~85% overlap but is clearly a different index). Talk to a CPA before doing this with significant amounts.
Practical recommendation for your situation
If you're building a $1M portfolio in a taxable account and want to set up TLH capability:

Primary holding: VOO (or whichever you prefer — they're functionally identical)
TLH partner: IVV or SPLG/SPYM — pick one and stick with it

When the market drops meaningfully (let's say -15% or more on your VOO position), you'd:

Sell VOO, realize the loss
Same day, buy equivalent dollar amount of IVV
Wait 31+ days

Does running the strategy across all three make sense?
Here's the more interesting question. There are three ways to think about this:
Option A — Three independent strategies. Run the framework separately on each ETF, each with its own dip reserve. Pros: clean rules. Cons: in a 2022-style correlated crash, all three trigger simultaneously, and you'd need 3x the total cash sitting in reserves.
Option B — One shared dip reserve, deploy on whichever triggers first. Single pool of cash, deploy into whatever ETF's tier just fired. Pros: capital-efficient. Cons: needs a rule for what to do when two ETFs trigger the same week. And in a correlated crash they almost always trigger together.
Option C — Target-allocation rebalancing. This is what I'd actually recommend, and it's quietly more powerful than the dip-tranche framework on multiple assets.
The idea: decide your target allocation in advance (e.g., 60% VOO / 20% QQQ / 20% VXUS). When VOO drops 20% but VXUS only drops 5%, VOO is now underweight in your portfolio, so the dip-tranche strategy directs more money to VOO — which is exactly what you'd want. When QQQ rips 40% and the others lag, QQQ is overweight; the strategy doesn't buy more QQQ (no drawdown trigger), and your regular DCA flows naturally into the underweight assets. The combination of target allocation + dip tranches is what most disciplined three-fund investors actually do.

1. QQQ already lives inside VOO. The Nasdaq-100's biggest companies (Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Amazon, Meta, Google, Tesla) are also the S&P 500's biggest holdings. Holding both VOO and QQQ means you're approximately 30% double-counted on those mega-caps.
