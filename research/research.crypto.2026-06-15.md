# HOUSEL LENS — BEHAVIORAL GUARDRAIL (non-voting). No BUY/SELL on BTC.

**(1) Is he about to make a behavioral error?**

Yes — and the tell is the *question itself*. "Did I miss it?" at $67k after asking at $61k is the language of regret and social comparison, not analysis. Six days, +10%, and the goalpost has already moved. Housel: *no one's crazy* — the FOMO is real, it's the very human pain of watching a thing you considered go up without you. But name it for what it is: the goalpost moving (§3 enough). $61k→$67k is a ~10% tick inside an asset that is **-46% from its ATH, below its 200d MA, sits in Extreme Fear (F&G 20), and which the crowd gives a 62% chance of touching $55k and ~50% of printing a $50k handle before year-end.** That is not a train leaving the station; that is noise inside a wide, downside-skewed range (the ±1SD 11-day move alone is ~±$4,300, i.e. the entire "did I miss it" gap is one standard deviation of weekly chop).

So the error risk is **both directions**, and they're the same disease — letting price action drive the clock:
- **FOMO-chase:** treating a +10% bounce as proof he was wrong to wait, and front-loading the whole stake into a green candle the night before an FOMC (Jun 16–17, SEP/dot-plot, ~1-day window). That is buying *because* it went up.
- **Anchor-and-freeze:** the mirror error — now anchored to "I should have bought at $61k," he refuses to act at $67k on principle and freezes, which is just a new anchor replacing the old forecast. The original "wait for CPI/FOMC" was a *reasonable* checkpoint, not a vow of paralysis; CPI has now printed (hot headline, cooling core) and FOMC is inside 48 hours. The checkpoint is essentially clearing.

The behavioral win is to make the next move *independent of whether $61k feels missed.* He didn't "miss" anything — there was never a guarantee of the trade, and a 10% move in BTC is a Tuesday.

**(2) Is a tranche scale-in behaviorally sound here?**

Yes — this is the structurally correct answer to *both* errors, which is exactly why it's the right instrument. A tranche plan is the behavioral antidote to the moving goalpost: it pre-commits the *process* so neither a green candle (FOMO) nor a red one (regret/freeze) gets a vote. Housel prizes **reasonable over rational** — the plan you can hold beats the optimal entry you'll abandon. A scale-in you can stick through a -30% lurch is worth more than a perfectly-timed lump you'll panic out of. It also matches the *game and horizon*: scaling in only makes sense if his horizon is multi-year compounding (where *time*, not entry tick, is the engine), not a swing trade. If he's actually playing a 6-week game, no entry method saves him. Confirm the horizon before the first tranche.

One guardrail on the mechanics: tranches should be **trigger-based, not feeling-based** — fixed dates or fixed price bands decided *now, in cold blood*, not "I'll add when it feels safe" (you never will) or "I'll add on the next green day" (that's FOMO with extra steps). The downside-skewed odds-ladder is a gift here: it says lower prints are *more* likely than the highs, so a scale-in that deliberately reserves dry powder for $55k/$50k/$45k is buying the path the crowd actually expects, not the one regret wants.

**(3) Is the sizing survivable to a further -50%?**

This is the only question that can end the conversation, and on the numbers as framed: **yes, by construction — IF the discipline holds.** The stake is drawn from the **~$0.5M risk capital, not the $1.5M house reserve.** That separation *is* the room for error — the reserve is the survival floor, untouchable, and Housel's whole keep-wealth doctrine is "be financially unbreakable." A -50% from $67k (to ~$33.5k — which is below even the 12.5%-odds $30k tail) impairs only risk capital that was, by definition, designated as risk-able. He does not get forced to sell anything, his life doesn't change, and compounding survives to run.

But "survivable" has a behavioral clause, not just an arithmetic one. It's only true if **(a)** the full intended BTC position is sized so that a -50% mark is a number he can look at without selling — i.e. size to the drawdown he can *emotionally* hold, which for most people is smaller than the one they can *financially* hold; and **(b)** he doesn't let the tranche plan quietly metastasize past the risk-capital boundary because "it's so cheap" on the way down (averaging down is disciplined *only* inside a pre-set max; past it, it's the gambler's escalation). The math is survivable. Whether *he* is survivable depends on pre-committing the max-BTC dollar cap today, before the first dollar goes in.

**(4) One guardrail rule:**

> **Write down, today and in cold blood, the total dollar cap for the BTC position (a slice of the $0.5M risk capital, never the $1.5M reserve) and a fixed tranche schedule — by date or by price band — then execute that schedule mechanically regardless of whether BTC is red or green. The plan gets the vote, not the candle. If a tranche trigger doesn't hit, the dry powder simply stays dry; "it bounced without me" is never a reason to accelerate.**
