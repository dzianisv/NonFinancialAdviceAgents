# Federal STR Tax — §280A Personal-Use Gate, §469 Gate, Depreciation Shell, and the Four Backstops

> Source: 26 U.S. Code §163/§164/§168/§280A/§465/§461/§469; Treas. Reg. §1.469-1T, §1.469-5T, §1.48-1
> (uscode.house.gov, law.cornell.edu). Distilled from the W-2 Real-Estate Tax Research run.
> **verified_on: 2026-07-13 — re-verify before use** (recovery periods, the §168(k) bonus rule keyed to
> acquisition date, and the §461(l) EBL cap can change by law; confirm for the actual property and year).

## The one-paragraph frame

For this W-2-loss strategy, an Airbnb-style **short-term rental (STR)** is analyzed as a **business
property only after it clears §280A**; it is not automatically outside the residence rules. Its tax
appeal — using paper depreciation losses against W-2 wages — is only available if the property first
clears the **§280A personal-use gate**, then the taxpayer walks through the **§469 passive-activity
gate** twice (not a rental activity, *and* material participation) and then survives four downstream
backstops. Keep it strictly separate from the PRIMARY_HOME analysis: the **full** personal-residence
itemized stack (mortgage-interest + SALT, no depreciation) and the **full** STR-business
depreciation-loss stack **cannot be combined on one property in the same year**. A single property *can*
have **mixed personal/rental use**, but that is a **separate §280A allocation regime** — the §280A(d)
residence test, the §280A(e) expense-allocation rule, and the §280A(g) <15-day rule — **outside this
simplified model**; route the day-count and allocation facts to a CPA/tax attorney.

## Step 0 — The §280A personal-use / vacation-home gate (runs BEFORE §469)

Before the §469 analysis even begins, **§280A** asks whether the dwelling is really a *residence*. Under
**26 U.S.C. §280A(d)(1)**, a taxpayer uses a unit as a **residence** if personal use exceeds the
**greater of (a) 14 days or (b) 10% of the days the unit is rented at a fair rental**. Personal use by
the owner, their family (per §267(c)(4)), or anyone renting below fair value counts.

- **Consequence of tripping §280A:** the unit is treated as a residence and **deductions attributable
  to the rental use are limited to the rental income** — the loss that the whole "offset my W-2" plan
  depends on is **disallowed** (excess carries forward). Personal-use days must be kept **below** the
  14-day / 10% line and **documented** for the STR-loss plan to survive.
- **Special <15-day rule [§280A(g)]:** if the unit is used as a residence and is **rented fewer than
  15 days** during the year, the rental income is **excluded from gross income** and **no rental
  deductions are allowed** — the opposite of a depreciation shell.
- **Mixed-use allocation [§280A(e)]:** when a dwelling has *both* personal and rental use, expenses must
  be **allocated between rental and personal days** under **§280A(e)**, and the rental deductions are
  then further limited by the §280A(d) residence cap. This mixed-use allocation is a **separate regime,
  outside this simplified STR model** — a single property having mixed use is **not impossible**, it is
  just governed by §280A(d)/(e)/(g) day-counting; route the day-count/allocation facts to a CPA/tax
  attorney.
- This gate is **independent of, and prior to, §469**: clearing §469's ≤7-day and material-participation
  tests is pointless if §280A has already capped the deductions at rental income.

## Step 1 — Default: rental losses are passive and disallowed [§469]

Under **26 U.S.C. §469**, a rental activity is *per se* passive, and passive losses cannot offset
non-passive income such as W-2 wages. Absent an exception, the STR loss is suspended and carried
forward. This is the default the "loophole" has to escape.

## Step 1a — Long-term-rental fallback: §469(i) $25k allowance & the §469(c)(7) REP exception

The Steps 2–3 STR exit doors (≤7-day average stay + material participation) apply to a **short-term**
rental. If the property is instead a **conventional long-term rental**, it stays a **rental activity**
that is **passive per se under §469(c)(2)**, and only two doors exist:

- **§469(i) active-participation allowance.** A taxpayer who **actively participates** in a rental (a
  lower bar than material participation) may use up to **$25,000** of rental loss against non-passive
  income. But the allowance **phases out at 50 cents per $1 of modified AGI above $100,000 and reaches
  $0 at $150,000 MAGI** — so for this skill's high-income W-2 persona it is **generally $0 /
  unavailable**.
- **§469(c)(7) real-estate-professional (REP) exception.** A rental is removed from the per-se-passive
  rule for a taxpayer who qualifies as a **real estate professional**. The test is **individual to one
  spouse**, who must satisfy **both** prongs in the tax year:
  1. **> 750 hours** of services in **real-property trades or businesses** in which the taxpayer
     materially participates, **and**
  2. **more than half (> 50%) of all the personal services** that spouse performs in *any* trade or
     business are in real-property trades or businesses.
  **Employee hours count toward these tests only if the spouse is a ≥ 5% owner** of the employer
  [§469(c)(7)(D)(ii)].
- **REP status alone is insufficient.** Even a qualified REP must **materially participate in the rental
  activity itself** (a §1.469-5T test) for its losses to be non-passive — or must **properly group the
  rentals into a single activity by electing aggregation under Reg. §1.469-9** so material participation
  is measured across the combined portfolio. Spousal participation **may** count toward the
  **material-participation** test, but **spouses do NOT pool their hours to meet the REP > 750 / > 50%
  prongs** — those are one-spouse tests.
- **Who realistically qualifies.** A **full-time W-2 employee** almost never meets the **> 50%** prong
  personally, because the W-2 job consumes most of their working hours. A **non-W-2 spouse** who runs the
  real-estate activity can qualify, but this is a **high-audit-scrutiny** position that requires
  **specialist review and defensible contemporaneous time records** — not a self-certified estimate.
- **Bottom line for a legal long-term rental:** its losses **cannot offset W-2 wages unless an exception
  such as REP + material participation (or the phased-out §469(i) allowance) applies.**

## Step 2 — Exit door #1: the ≤7-day average-stay exception [Treas. Reg. §1.469-1T]

**Treas. Reg. §1.469-1T(e)(3)** excludes an activity from the definition of a "rental activity" when
the **average period of customer use is 7 days or less**. Clear this and the activity is no longer
governed by the rental *per se* rule — but it is not yet non-passive. You still need Step 3.

- Compute the average stay across all bookings for the year; a handful of long stays can push the
  average over 7 days and slam this door shut.
- A ≤ 30-day average with *significant personal services* is a separate, narrower exception — the
  ≤ 7-day path is the conservative one to model.

## Step 3 — Exit door #2: material participation [Treas. Reg. §1.469-5T]

Even after Step 2, losses are usable against wages only if the taxpayer **materially participates**.
**Treas. Reg. §1.469-5T(a)** provides the tests; the taxpayer needs to meet **one** of them. The
most-cited and most-defensible:

1. **> 500 hours** of participation in the activity during the year.
2. Participation is **substantially all** the participation of everyone in the activity.
3. **> 100 hours** *and* no other individual participated more.
4. Significant-participation activities aggregating > 500 hours.
5. Material participation in **any 5 of the prior 10** years.
6. Personal-service-activity prior-3-year test.
7. Facts-and-circumstances regular/continuous/substantial participation.

**Proof standard [Treas. Reg. §1.469-5T(f)(4)]:** contemporaneous daily time logs are **not legally
required**. Participation "may be established by **any reasonable means**," expressly including
**appointment books, calendars, or narrative summaries** identifying the services performed and the
approximate hours. A **contemporaneous task/time record is nonetheless strongly recommended as
audit-defense best practice** — a reconstructed year-end estimate is far weaker evidence, even though
it is not statutorily barred.

**Management company nuance:** using a professional manager does **not** automatically defeat material
participation or the **>500-hour** test — Test 1 counts only the taxpayer's own hours, so someone else
working more is irrelevant to it. What a manager makes harder are the **substantially-all** test
(§1.469-5T(a)(2)), the **>100-hours-and-no-one-else-participated-more** test (a)(3), and the
**facts-and-circumstances** test (a)(7) — and under §1.469-5T(f)(2), management-type work can be
discounted where others are paid to manage or spend more time managing. Model this as a real operating
commitment, not a checkbox.

## Step 4 — The depreciation shell: 39-year transient-use, NOT 27.5-year [§168]

A dwelling used on a **transient basis** is excluded from "residential rental property," so an STR is
depreciated as **nonresidential real property over 39 years — not the 27.5-year residential-rental
period** [§168]. Using 27.5 years for a transient-use STR shell is the common error; the conservative,
defensible period is **39 years**.

**Illustrative shell on a normalized $1,000,000 property (an illustration, not a promise):**

| Component | Amount | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Land (assume 20%) | $200,000 | Non-depreciable |
| Depreciable basis | $800,000 | — |
| Cost-seg reclassification (~25%) | $200,000 | Short-life tangible personal property [§1.48-1] → bonus-eligible |
| Remaining building | $600,000 | 39-year straight line ≈ $15,385/yr |
| **First-year shell (bonus on $200k + $15,385)** | **≈ $215,385** | ~35% marginal → **≈ $75,385 timing benefit** |

**Cost segregation** ( [§1.48-1] ) reclassifies building components into **tangible personal property**
(short recovery life, bonus-eligible) distinct from **structural components** (long life). A study is
what supports the reclassification percentage — do not assume 25%; it is property-specific.

## Step 5 — Bonus depreciation: 100% is currently permanent — verify by acquisition date

As of **2026-07-13**, the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), Pub. L. 119-21, §70301** amended
**§168(k)** to make **100% bonus depreciation permanent** for qualified property **acquired after
2025-01-19** and placed in service thereafter. Property **acquired on or before 2025-01-19** stays on
the older transition/phase-down schedule (the pre-OBBBA step-down). Two rules still bind:

- **Re-verify the acquisition date and the then-current law** for the actual property — the 100% figure
  is tied to *acquisition* timing, and future legislation can change it, so treat it as
  `[UNVERIFIED — confirm acquisition date and current §168(k)]` until pinned.
- **Conventions and eligibility still apply:** mid-month / mid-quarter conventions reduce the first-year
  deduction, and only qualified property (e.g. the cost-seg-reclassified short-life personal property)
  is bonus-eligible — the 39-year building shell is not.

## Step 6 — The four backstops that shrink the shell (always list them)

Even a valid §469-nonpassive STR loss is throttled by four limits, in rough order of application:

1. **Basis limitation** — losses are limited to the taxpayer's adjusted basis in the activity.
2. **§465 at-risk** — losses are further limited to the amount the taxpayer has economically **at
   risk** (cash + recourse debt), excluding non-recourse financing that is not qualified.
3. **§461(l) excess business loss (EBL)** — a noncorporate taxpayer's net business loss usable against
   **non-business** income (like wages) is **capped** each year; the excess is carried forward as a
   net operating loss (NOL). This is the limit most likely to cap a large first-year STR shell against
   a high salary. The cap amount is inflation-indexed — re-verify for the year.
4. **Depreciation recapture** — at sale, accelerated deductions are recaptured: **§1245** on personal
   property reclassified by cost seg, **§1250** unrecaptured gain (taxed up to 25%) on the building.
   This is why the shell is a **timing** benefit, not free money — it front-loads deductions and
   pays some back at exit.

## What to tell the user

- The STR path converts a residence decision into an **active-business decision** governed by
  hour-counting, defensible recordkeeping (contemporaneous logs recommended but not legally required),
  and local permitting — a different risk profile entirely.
- Every dollar figure is an **illustration**, not a promise. The real number depends on MAGI, actual
  rental income/expenses, the cost-seg study, the acquisition-date §168(k) bonus rule, the §461(l) cap,
  and eventual recapture.
- Feasibility (permit/HOA/zoning — see `insurance-and-feasibility.md`) is the **binding** constraint,
  not the depreciation math.
- Close with: *Educational only — not tax, legal, or financial advice. Confirm every number with a
  licensed CPA or tax attorney for your actual facts and placed-in-service year.*
